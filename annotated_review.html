Personalization · Design Review

Why each screen works the way it does

A walkthrough of the three personalization screens, organized by the reasoning behind each decision rather than by build history. Items tagged OPEN are known gaps worth a ticket before this goes further — everything else reflects something already present in the screen next to it.

1. Personalization home
Try the New user toggle, the nudge card, and the kudos card
Tone & framing
1
Thread motif, not a feed
Cards are connected by a visible line rather than stacked as a generic feed — signals these are one continuous read on the user's day, not unrelated notifications competing for attention.
2
Briefing leads with pattern, not score
"4 of 6 rings" and "third day this week" describe behavior in context. No grade, no implied judgment.
3
Trend card explicitly avoids a single number
States "no single score here" directly in-product, and gives the Tuesday dip context (offsite) rather than leaving it to read as a failure.
4
Missed-task reasons are descriptive, not judgmental
"Pushed 3 times," "no time block set" — observations about the task, never framed as something wrong with the person.
Transparency & reversibility
5
"Why am I seeing this" expands inline
Tapping it shows the actual reasoning ("based on 14 days...") right there, rather than being a transparency gesture with nowhere to go.
6
"Not now" carries equal weight to "Move it," and both are undoable
Dismissal is a first-class action, not a smaller afterthought — and a mis-tap on either button isn't permanent. Both confirm what happened and offer Undo for a few seconds.
7
Kudos works the same way: Send or Skip, either reversible
One friend, one moment — a real "Skip," not a leaderboard, and Undo on both actions so a fast tap doesn't lock in a choice you didn't mean to make.
8
Nudge card shows its place in a queue ("1 of 2 today")
Sets honest expectations that more exist, rather than the home screen silently picking one and hiding the rest.
Accessibility & clarity
9
Color is never the only signal
Each node marker pairs its color with a distinct shape and glyph (✦ briefing, ! nudge, ~ trend, · missed, ♥ kudos), and text uses a darker violet that clears AA contrast against light backgrounds.
10
An "insight" chip and an "open task" chip used to look identical
"Mornings work for you" (an observation) and "Gym ring open" (an action) now have distinct styling — the ring chip has its own color, icon, and a real tap target — plus a caption spelling out the difference. Resolved: tapping it used to close the ring directly in place, which was a mockup shortcut. Closing a ring is likely more than a single tap of state (duration, reps, streak), so it shouldn't resolve inline the way a nudge or kudos can. Tapping now opens a short explainer modal instead, stating plainly that the real interaction is a deep link into the Rings tab scoped to that ring — showing the intended destination rather than faking a completion the mockup can't actually represent.
11
A modal for a design decision, not a real product surface
This confirm-style dialog isn't meant to model UI that ships — it's a placeholder that documents an intentional deferral for whoever builds this next, in the same spirit as the "mockup shortcut" notes elsewhere in this doc. Dismissible by the "Got it" button or by tapping outside it.
12
Day-one users get an explanation, not a blank or fabricated screen
The New user state explains why each card is empty and what unlocks it, instead of inventing a pattern that doesn't exist yet.
Every card resolves to an action
13
Trend and missed-task cards now do something, not just report something
The other three cards already end in a real action — move a nudge, close a ring, send kudos. These two were read-only. "This week's pattern" gets a "See full month" toggle that swaps in an actual second data view (4 weekly averages instead of 7 daily bars), reusing the same expand pattern as "Why am I seeing this" and "Learn more" rather than adding a decorative button. "What got missed" gets a per-item action tied to the specific reason it lists, not a generic one — the repeatedly-pushed expense report gets "Move to today," the dentist call flagged as having no time block gets "Add a time block" — and both resolve with the same confirm/undo pattern already used for nudges and kudos.
Open
14
Missed task names are visible on a shared or unlocked screenOPEN
"Submit expense report" sitting on a home screen is fine alone, less fine on a lock-screen preview or a screen-share. Worth a "blur sensitive task names" setting.
15
Jordan's streak is shown to someone else without Jordan's consentOPEN
The kudos card surfaces another person's activity. That needs its own opt-in on Jordan's side, not just the viewer's settings. Harder to mock up meaningfully here — this view is Maya's alone, so there's no real surface in this file to grant or preview Jordan's consent from.
2. Personalization onboarding
Opens on a short expectations screen, then a single-step wizard — Back / Continue / Skip move between steps, with state preserved
Before you start
16
A welcome screen sets expectations before question one
Shows a sample briefing and a sample nudge side by side so "personalization" means something concrete, then a single privacy/timing line underneath covers when to expect results (briefing tonight, nudges in about a week) and confirms it's visible only to you. Topbar is stripped down to a back button and a progress bar; no second text label competing with the "Before you start" tag. A "Skip setup — use defaults" link jumps straight to confirm for anyone who'd rather not answer anything at all.
17
Progress bar carries over, but stays unfilled here
Two ways to show progress across a welcome screen: add a 5th segment for it, or reuse the same 4-segment bar from the question steps with nothing filled in yet. Went with the latter — a 5th segment would contradict the "four quick questions" framing in the copy above and force every step's "Step X of 4" label to be rewritten. An empty bar reads clearly as "the four questions haven't started," and the first segment fills the moment you land on question one.
Tone & framing
18
"Skip for now" is on every step, and worded so it's clearly reversible
Personalization is additive, never a gate — "you can set this up later" removes the fear that skipping forfeits it permanently.
19
No right-answer framing on planning style
The subhead states directly that there's no correct option — pre-empts the instinct to answer how Kindred "wants" rather than how someone actually behaves.
20
Frequency stays a slider; tone became sample-cards
"How much" and "how it sounds" are still two separate settings — someone may want light-touch + cheerful, or frequent + quiet. But tone no longer asks someone to place themselves on an abstract Quiet↔Cheerful scale: it shows the same nudge written three ways and asks which one sounds like them. Selecting a card sets the same underlying value the slider used to; frequency keeps its draggable track with keyboard arrow-key support.
21
"Nudges are suggestions, not alerts," with a stated daily ceiling
Sets expectations before a single nudge has been sent, and points to a concrete limit in settings so "light touch" isn't just a vibe.
Trust through the flow
22
An autosave note at the end removes the "did I lose this?" worry
"Your answers save automatically" removes the anxiety of losing progress mid-flow. Per-step time estimates were cut — they read as filler once the welcome screen already sets expectations for the whole flow.
23
A real "Learn more" panel, and an editable, live summary before commit
"Learn more about how this data is used" expands inline with the longer explanation. The confirm screen reads back your actual choices from every prior step — not placeholder text — and each item links straight back to where it came from.
24
Deselecting every focus time has an explicit fallback
"No pattern yet — we'll use your activity instead" replaces a silent, unexplained zero-selection state.
Open
25
No calendar-conflict awareness in nudge timingOPEN
Onboarding asks when someone's sharpest, but a later nudge ("Move to 8:40am?") could land on a meeting. Worth checking calendar availability, not just historical pattern.
3. Personalization settings
Every row is interactive — try "Pause personalization," now sitting above "Turn off"
Trust in your personalization
26
"From you" vs. "Inferred" tags, and corrections always win
Distinguishes what the user explicitly stated from what personalization observed — and states plainly that a correction overrides what's inferred, so fixing it feels like correcting a guess, not arguing with the system.
27
"See exactly what data this uses" is a real, working link
Opens an explanation of what's self-reported vs. inferred and confirms nothing leaves the account unless the person taps Export.
Fatigue & scoring
28
"Auto-reduce when ignored" is a visible, working toggle
The fatigue-protection guardrail isn't just backend behavior — it's something the user can see is on, building confidence it won't nag.
29
Score display is off by default
Trend view is the default experience; the single productivity number is opt-in, reversing the typical "score-first" pattern of habit apps.
Reversibility & commitment levels
30
Four distinct levels: Need a break, Pause personalization, Turn off, Delete
"Need a break" pauses nudges only — briefings and reports keep going. "Pause personalization" (new, sits above "Turn off") stops everything for a chosen duration and resumes automatically. "Turn off" is indefinite until manually resumed. "Delete" is the only one that's permanent. Each is a meaningfully different commitment, not variations of one button.
31
Destructive or paused states always confirm and show their effect
Confirming a pause, turn-off, or delete opens a sheet stating exactly what's kept and lost, then shows a banner reflecting what actually happened — pause and turn-off offer Resume / Turn back on and dim the sections that stop updating; delete shows its own message ("patterns and history are gone, starting fresh") since resuming isn't really what happened there, and personalization stays active rather than appearing off.
Accessibility & control
32
Toggle state never relies on color alone
The knob's position and the background fill both change, so state reads correctly even for users who can't distinguish the on/off color difference.
33
Light / Dark / Auto is a real, working theme swap
Relevant for migraine, light sensitivity, and plain preference — selecting Dark actually re-themes the screen rather than just marking a button as selected.
34
Friend sharing defaults to off, kudos suggestions default to on
Low-stakes social (suggesting kudos) and higher-stakes social (sharing your own progress/scores) are split into separate toggles with different defaults.
Data rights
35
Export sits above the destructive options
Someone shouldn't have to choose between keeping a copy of their data and deleting it — exporting is always available first.
Open
36
"Your personalization" still has no audit trail
Resolved, partially: added a "See what's changed" sheet next to "See exactly what data this uses," listing each change to planning style, tone, and focus times with a timestamp — and it's wired to real interactions, not static copy: cycling a value or toggling a focus-time chip in this same screen adds a live "Just now" entry you can see immediately. What this doesn't cover: the specific signals behind an inferred trait (which days, which tasks) — that's a deeper explainability feature, not a change log, and is still worth its own ticket.

Considered, not yet designed

Real problems worth a ticket, but solving them changes scope beyond a screen-level fix — flagging so they don't get lost between this review and a spec.

  • Loading and network-error states for all three screens
  • Push notification permission flow (OS-level, before any nudge can fire)
  • Priority/ordering logic when multiple nudges qualify the same day
  • Calendar-conflict checking before suggesting a specific time
  • Blurring or hiding sensitive task names on lock screen / screen-share
  • Friend-side consent for surfacing their streaks in someone else's kudos card
  • Customizable kudos message instead of a single canned send
  • Cross-device sync messaging (is this account-level or per-device?)
  • Longer translated strings breaking chip/button layout (i18n)
  • RTL layout mirroring for the thread motif and card alignment
  • Age-appropriate defaults if the product is ever opened to minors
  • An actual screen behind "See exactly what data this uses," not just a sheet
  • "Add friends" in the New user state isn't wired to anything yet

Guardrails these decisions map back to

The recurring principles behind the choices above, grouped so they're easy to check new features against.

Tone & framing

  • Describe, don't judge — patterns and reasons are stated neutrally, never as a verdict on the person
  • No single score by default — trends first, a number only if asked for

Reversibility

  • Undo where it's cheap — nudge and kudos actions, in particular
  • Confirm where it's not — anything destructive gets a sheet, not a tap
  • Graduated commitment — pause, turn off, and delete are not the same decision

Accessibility

  • Never color-only — shape, glyph, or position always carries state too
  • AA contrast minimum, real touch targets, and working theme control

Honesty about state

  • Cold start is designed, not blank — day-one users get an explanation
  • Queues and effects are visible — "1 of 2," a banner when paused, a real Export
  • Transparency links resolve — no link that dead-ends
