Personalization · Design Alternative 2 of 3 · Deck

Why the deck works the way it does

A walkthrough of the swipeable card-stack alternative to the personalization thread, organized by the reasoning behind each decision. This reviews one screen only — the interaction shell around the same five underlying cards, not the onboarding or settings screens, which are unchanged. Items tagged OPEN are known gaps worth a ticket. Items tagged NEW are decisions this format required that didn't exist in the thread version.

What the deck trades away

The thread let you skim all five cards at a glance and act on whichever one you wanted, in any order, or scroll past the rest. The deck forces one deliberate decision at a time — nothing gets passively skimmed past unread, but a quick glance at "everything today" is no longer possible, and it wouldn't scale gracefully much past six cards.

What it's built for instead

Every card in this feature already ends in some kind of action. The deck makes resolving that action the whole mechanic, rather than one option among several ways to interact with a card. It's a deliberate bet that "one decision, then the next" beats "a report you scan" for a feature that's fundamentally a short daily to-do list of its own.

Tone & framing
1
Swipe stamps and buttons stay violet / sage — never red / green
A left-right decision format invites a stock "reject vs. accept" color scheme. Instead every card keeps the color it already had in the thread model: violet for the system's own suggestions (nudge, missed tasks), sage for the social one (kudos), and a neutral gray for whichever direction means "not now." Nothing reads as a wrong answer.
2
Missed-task and dismissal copy is unchanged from the thread
"Pushed 3 times," "no time block set," "won't ask again this week" — same neutral, descriptive language, just now living in a full-bleed card instead of a compact one.
3
Briefing and trend never get forced into an accept/reject shape
Swiping either direction on a read-only card does the same thing — advance — and both stamps just say "Next." The deck format's left/right mechanic doesn't get applied somewhere it doesn't belong just for consistency's sake.
Every card resolves to a decision
4
Missed tasks split into two separate cardsNEW
The thread showed both missed items in one card, each with its own small CTA. The deck can only resolve one thing per swipe, so "Submit expense report" and "Call dentist" are now two cards, each with its own specific action — "Move to today" and "Add a time block" respectively — instead of one generic pair.
5
Decision cards needed a real negative action, so one was inventedNEW
The thread's missed-task items only had a positive link ("Move to today") with no explicit opposite — you just didn't tap it. A card that must resolve one way or the other can't leave a direction blank, so the left swipe now means something specific: "Leave for now." This is a genuine new decision point the deck format introduces, not a port of something that existed before.
6
Local counters ("1 of 2 today," "1 of 2") stay separate from the deck's own count
The nudge card's "1 of 2 today" describes a queue of nudges that exists independent of this deck — there may be a second nudge waiting elsewhere today. The missed-task cards' "1 of 2" describes their own pair. Neither is the same number as "3 of 6" in the header, and none of them are conflated into one counter.
Reversibility & the review list
7
Undo is a toast, not an inline row, and it doesn't expire on a timer
Once a card leaves the stack it's off-screen, so the thread's inline confirm/undo row (which stayed visible next to the card itself) doesn't have anywhere to live. A toast takes its place — but, matching the thread's own precedent, it stays up until you tap Undo or resolve the next card, rather than auto-hiding after a few seconds and quietly becoming permanent.
8
A same-session "to revisit" sheetNEW
The design-alternatives brief left this as an open question — should a skipped card reappear tomorrow, or land in a same-session review list? This builds the second option: every "Not now" / "Leave for now" / "Skip" adds an entry to a ⟲ pill next to the progress dots, which opens a short list with a working "Reconsider" action per item.
9
Reconsidering re-queues a card instead of losing it
Tapping "Reconsider" removes the item from the revisit list and puts it back at the very front of the stack — it becomes the next card you see, with a fresh set of buttons, rather than just being marked as changed your mind somewhere in the background.
10
Only the most recent action is undo-ableOPEN
Because only one card is visible at a time, the toast can only ever refer to the last thing you did — resolve two cards in a row and the first one's Undo is gone (though a left-swipe is still reachable through the revisit sheet; a right-swipe is not). The thread's model didn't have this limitation, since every card's undo stayed visible at once. Flagging rather than solving, since a multi-level undo stack is a bigger interaction to design well.
Accessibility & reliability
11
The buttons under the stack are the primary path, not a fallback
The brief itself calls gesture-only risky for a task app. Every card, decision or not, has real buttons doing the exact same thing a swipe does — nothing here is reachable only by dragging.
12
Real <button> elements throughout, not div + tabindex + role shim
The thread mockups used clickable divs with a hand-rolled keydown handler to make them keyboard-operable. Everything actionable in the deck — state pills, action-row buttons, the why-link, reconsider rows — is a native button, so focus order, Enter/Space activation, and screen-reader semantics come from the browser instead of being reimplemented.
13
Reduced-motion turns physics into a snap
Spring-back, fly-off, and the card-advancing cascade are all real CSS transitions, and a prefers-reduced-motion query collapses their duration to effectively zero. The interaction still works exactly the same way — it just doesn't animate for anyone who's asked their system not to.
14
Arrow keys are a keyboard-native swipeNEW
Left/Right on the keyboard resolve the top card in that direction, same as a drag or a tap — one more way in besides the visible buttons, for anyone driving the deck without a pointer.
15
Tapping "why am I seeing this" doesn't get swallowed by the drag gesture
The whole card is a drag target, which could easily make small inline controls (the why-link, the trend's month toggle, the ring chip) fire a swipe by accident. A short movement threshold means a tap is only ever treated as a tap — dragging only engages once the pointer has actually moved a few pixels.
16
Peeking cards keep their glyph, not just their color
The two cards visible behind the top one are abstracted down to a label and a small badge — but that badge still uses the thread's own shape-plus-glyph coding (✦ briefing, ! nudge, ~ trend, · missed, ♥ kudos), so what's coming next is identifiable without reading full text.
Honesty about state
17
Cold start is one explanatory card, not five empty onesNEW
A literal per-card port of the thread's "New user" state would mean swiping through five separate "nothing here yet" cards before reaching the end — busywork with no payoff, regardless of interaction model. A single card explains what's missing and points at "Add friends," matching the spirit (though not the letter) of the brief's own cold-start guidance for its other alternatives.
18
"You're all caught up" links somewhere real
The end card doesn't just dead-end — its two buttons open the same "this would open X" pattern already used for the ring chip, pointing at Tasks and Rings, and it notes how many items are still sitting in the revisit list if any are.
19
Dots and a counter always show where you are
Six dots track resolved / current / upcoming, plus a plain "3 of 6 today" — the deck never asks you to just remember how far you've gotten.
Open
20
Revisit items don't persist past this sessionOPEN
The brief's own question — reappear tomorrow, or same-session list — is answered here with "same-session list," but that's a real product decision, not just a UI one. Closing the app with two items still in "to revisit" currently means they're gone; whether they should roll into tomorrow's deck instead is unresolved.
21
Card order is fixedOPEN
Briefing, nudge, trend, missed ×2, kudos — always in that order. The brief flags this directly: order becomes a real design decision once cards must be resolved one at a time. No priority logic exists yet for what should lead when, say, two nudges qualify the same day.
22
Swipe direction isn't mirrored for RTLOPEN
Right = positive, left = negative is a strong left-to-right assumption baked into both the drag physics and the button order. An Arabic or Hebrew layout would need this reasoned through, not just flipped automatically.
23
Gesture-first has a real cost even with every safety net in placeOPEN
Fallback buttons, keyboard arrows, and reduced-motion handling all soften this, but a drag-to-resolve interaction is still more physically demanding than scrolling and tapping a link — worth keeping in mind for anyone with limited fine motor control, even though nothing here is gesture-only.
24
Missed task names are visible on a shared or unlocked screenOPEN
Carried over unresolved from the thread model — "Submit expense report" filling most of a card is more exposed here, not less, since it's the only thing on screen.
25
Jordan's streak is shown without Jordan's consentOPEN
Also carried over unresolved — this needs its own opt-in on Jordan's side, not just Maya's settings. Same limitation as the thread model; the deck doesn't make it better or worse.

Considered, not yet designed

Real problems worth a ticket, but solving them changes scope beyond this screen — flagging so they don't get lost between this review and a spec.

  • Loading and network-error states for the deck
  • Where a same-day second nudge goes — a seventh card, or held for tomorrow
  • Priority/ordering logic when more cards qualify than fit comfortably in one sitting
  • Whether "to revisit" items roll into tomorrow's deck or reset at midnight
  • Cross-device sync of in-session revisit state
  • Calendar-conflict checking before suggesting a specific nudge time
  • Blurring or hiding sensitive task names on lock screen / screen-share
  • Friend-side consent for surfacing their streaks in someone else's kudos card
  • Customizable kudos message instead of a single canned send
  • Longer translated strings breaking the two-button row or the swipe stamps (i18n)
  • Full RTL layout and swipe-direction mirroring
  • Age-appropriate defaults if the product is ever opened to minors

Guardrails these decisions map back to

Mostly the same principles as the thread model's review — carried over deliberately, since this is a shell change around the same feature, not a different product.

Tone & framing

  • Describe, don't judge — carried over unchanged in every card's copy
  • No accept/reject color coding — violet and sage stay tied to who's speaking, not to "yes" and "no"

Reversibility

  • Undo where it's cheap — a non-expiring toast for the last action
  • A path back for what's harder to reverse — the revisit sheet, for this session at least

Accessibility

  • Gesture is never the only path — buttons and arrow keys do everything a drag does
  • Motion is opt-out-able, and native controls carry their own semantics

Honesty about state

  • Cold start is designed, not blank — even when that means one card instead of a literal port
  • Position is always visible — dots, a counter, and a real end state
  • Nothing dead-ends — the caught-up card and the ring chip both point somewhere
